  1. The first bar graph addresses the 1st question by showing the number of games per year from 2020 down to 2011. The second graph, the map, shows the top 10 winning nations and you can hover over the blue countries to see their winning %. The third graph, the lollipop chart, incorporates the toggle button and predicts which team will win the 2022 World Cup based on data from the past two world cups (2018 and 2014) based on first Winning % out of those 2 World Cups or Victory Strength (how much the winner won by for each of the past 2 World Cup tournament games). The prediction shows as the title of the graph. I chose these two metrics because we should bet on teams that have won most of their games in the past as that shows a good performance record and a higher victory strength means they didn't just barely win by chance but are actually a better team.
  2. D3 was helpful because it allowed me to express the data in a geographical way on the map, which is visually beneficial. Also, the hover function allowed me to express the winning % information without cluttering the map area by showing it all at once. Thirdly, the toggle button function helped to visually see the direct change in the World Cup winner prediction based on the two different metrics and it's easy to toggle back and forth for comparison.
  3. D3 would not be the best tool for creating a visualization if the visualization creator is not well-versed in coding and needs a quick and easy way to visualize data. That's where something like Tableau would be better since it still represents data in numerical and organized graphs but you don't have to build everything from the ground up in a complex manner. Another reason you wouldn't use D3 is if you have many SVG elements needed to show your data. Creating the js and html code to account for all those graphs would be very painstaking and inefficient. Finally, D3 wouldn't be the best tool if the target audience learns better through audio or some other form of information platform that doesn't rely as heavily on just one human sense. For example, showing video or more complicated multimedia components as part of the data would be hard through D3.

    4. Accessible Data Visualization


  4. Users who have full visual ability and digest visual information well would find this dashboard accessbile. As referenced in "A Comprehensive Guide to Accessible Data Visualization", color blind or blind users may find visual dashboards more difficult to use. I chose the blue color for the map off of the color blind friendly website, but I could do that for all the colors in my graphs to make sure none of the graphs are harder to see than others. The article also provides color contrast guidelines to ensure better legibility for users which I could incorporate. Both articles also reference assistive technology, such as a screen reader which only reads the text on the screen, which means my hover feature for the map would be hidden for certain users. I could add the hover information somewhere on the side of the map on the screen in addition to the hover feature so that users with screen readers can still get all the necessary information off the visualization.
  5. I could have added the hover information on the side of the map when I was implementing the hover feature. I didn't do so because I didn't know about users with screen readers and how the hover information would be hidden from them, but now I know! I also could have checked the other colors in my graph when I was using the color blind friendly blue color from the website.
